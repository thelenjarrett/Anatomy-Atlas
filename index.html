Muscles of the Head

Medial Pterygoid

Origin: Sphenoid

Insertion: Ramus, Mandible

Function: Elevation and pronation of mandible

Innervation: Mandibular nerve (CN V3)

The Medial Pterygoid consists of two heads, each with their own origin and insertion. The superficial head originates from the maxillary tuberosity and the pyramidal process of the palatine bone. The deep head originates from the medial surface of the lateral pterygoid plate of sphenoid bone and from the pyramidal process of the palatine bone. Both heads insert to the triangular impression found on the medial surface of the ramus and angle of mandible. Because of the insertion on the angle of the mandible it is unsurprising the functions of the medial pterygoid deal with moving the mandible, but specifically attaching to the angle allows the medial pterygoid to move the mandible forward.

Common injuries can include lockjaw, strains, and possibly trauma. Being so close to the CN V3 nerve, dental procedures involving improper anesthesia application can lead to lockjaw. Being one of the primary chewing muscles, the medial pterygoid is susceptible to strain. Outside trauma to the jaw can impact the medial pterygoid despite being behind bone.

Lateral Pterygoid

Origin: Maxilla, Palatine bone, Sphenoid bone

Insertion: Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ), Mandible

Function: Protrusion and depression of mandible

Innervation: Mandibular nerve (CN V3)

The Lateral Pterygoid consists of two heads, each with their own origin and insertion. The superior head originates from the infratemporal crest of greater wing of the sphenoid bone. The inferior head originates from the lateral surface of lateral pterygoid plate of the sphenoid bone. Both heads insert at the joint capsule of the temporomandibular joint. Because of this insertion, the lateral pterygoid allows rotation of the jaw if you only contract one side at a time.

Common injuries can include strains and displacement. Because the lateral pterygoid is active in nearly all jaw functions it is very susceptible to strains and knots. Attachment to the TMJ makes injuries of the lateral pterygoid often manifest in other parts of the head like the ear. This insertion also makes it possible to displace the articular disc if the lateral pterygoid is flexed hard enough.

Procerus

Origin: Nasal Bone

Insertion: Skin of glabella

Function: Depresses medial eyebrow and wrinkles glabella

Innervation: Facial nerve (CN VII)

The Procerus is a small fan shaped muscle between the eyebrows. Its origin at the nasal bone along with its insertion at the glabella means it lowers the inner half of the eyebrows when flexed. This makes the procerus capable of creating a wide array of facial exprressions. The levator labii superioris attaches to the lower part of the procerus, while the corrugator supercilii attaches to the upper part.

Common injuries can include strains, surgeries, and nerve damage. Constant strain of the eyes due to poor vision or screen use can strain cause strains. The glabella is a common site for botox injections and an improper application can harm the procerus. Any nerve damage to the CN VII can affect the procerus.

Nasalis

Origin: Maxilla

Insertion: Dorsum of nose, skin of ala

Function: Flaring Nostrils

Innervation: Facial nerve (CN VII)

The Nasalis is a pair of muscles on either side of the nose. The outer part of the nasalis comprresses the openings of the nose, while the front part dilates them. The outer part blends with the bottom of the procerus and the front part is right next to the depressor septi.

Common injuries can include muscle and nerve damage. Facial suregeries such as rhinoplasties can damage the nasalis. Damage to the CN VII nerve can affect the nasalis as well.

Mentalis

Origin: Mandible

Insertion: Mentolabial Sulcus

Function: Elevation, evertion, and protrusion of lower lip

Innervation: Facial nerve (CN VII)

The Mentalis is a pair of muscles that originates under the outermost incisor and inserts into the skin of your chin. Because of this origination, the mentalis can raise the lower lip causing eversion and protrusion, seen in drinking. The upper part of the mentalis is covered by the depressor labii inferioris.

Common injuries can include nerve damage and strains. Dental procedures and surgeries like genioplasties can affect the mentalis. Those with recessed chins will find the mentalis needs to strain to fully pull the lower lip into a closed position.

Depressor Labii Inferioris

Origin: Mandible

Insertion: Lower Lip

Function: Depress lower lip

Innervation: Facial nerve (CN VII)

The Depressor Labii Inferioris is a pair of muscles that originates at the oblique line of the mandible where the fibers then go up and to the middle before inserting at the skin of the lower lip. This insertion angle allows the lower lips to move down and to the middle. The inferolateral part of the depressor labii inferioris lies underneath the depressor anguli oris, while the superolateral lies above the mentalis.

Common injuries can include nerve damage and surgical malfunctions. The marginal mandibular branch of the facial nerve is what makes the depressor labii inferioris function and is susceptible to injury during procedures like liposuctions or facelifts. Botox applications have been known to drift and affect the depressor labii inferioris.
